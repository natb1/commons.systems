# Running Mythic Bastionland

I ran my first session of Mythic Bastionland last month, and it rewired how I think about prep.

## Knights From Nothing

The Knight-generation procedure is the real hook. You roll a few tables and out comes a character with a name, a calling, a signature weapon, and — critically — a debt that ties them to the realm. No twenty-page backstory. No "my character would never do that" arguments. The tables give you just enough to play and leave the rest to emerge at the table.

My group generated three Knights in about fifteen minutes. One owed a season of service to a border lord. Another carried a stolen relic they couldn't return. The third had sworn an oath to find a missing heir. We hadn't rolled initiative once and already had three sessions worth of direction.

## The Realm Map Does the Work

The realm map is the GM's best friend. You place holdings, dungeons, and landmarks on a hex grid before play starts. Then you connect them with roads, rivers, and rumors. When the Knights set off from their starting holding, the map tells you what's nearby, who controls it, and what trouble is brewing.

I stopped planning encounters entirely. Instead I looked at the map, checked which factions had conflicting goals, and let the Knights walk into the middle of it. The procedural tables handle the specifics: weather, encounters, NPC reactions. My job shrank from "design tonight's adventure" to "look at the map and say yes."

## What Clicks

Three things make the system work:

**Scars replace leveling.** When a Knight takes Critical Damage, they gain a Scar — a permanent change that's both narrative and mechanical. Progress comes from surviving, not from XP grinding. Players started treating danger as an opportunity rather than an obstacle.

**The Myth changes everything.** Each season of play introduces a Myth — a large-scale event that reshapes the realm. A dragon wakes up. A plague spreads. A rival kingdom invades. The Myth gives the Knights something bigger than themselves to respond to, and it means the realm never feels static.

**Rulings over rules.** The core mechanic is simple: roll a d20, try to get under your stat. Everything else is negotiation between the table and the fiction. Combat is fast and lethal enough that players look for alternatives — bribing the guard, collapsing the bridge, negotiating with the dragon. The system gets out of the way and lets the story happen.

If you've bounced off trad fantasy RPGs because of the prep burden, Mythic Bastionland is worth a look. The tools do the heavy lifting. You just show up and play.
