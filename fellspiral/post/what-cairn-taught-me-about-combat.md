# What Cairn Taught Me About Combat

I ran D&D for years before switching to Cairn. The biggest change wasn't the setting or the tone — it was how fights work.

## No To-Hit Rolls

In Cairn, attacks always hit. You roll damage and subtract it from the target's HP, then STR once HP is gone. That single design choice changes everything about how combat feels at the table.

There's no whiffing. No "I swing and miss" four rounds in a row. Every action a player takes in combat has a consequence. When you swing a sword, something happens. When a monster claws at you, you feel it. The question stops being "did I hit?" and becomes "what do I do about the damage I just took?"

This sounds like it would make combat faster and more violent, and it does. But the real effect is upstream: players stop treating fights as the default problem-solving tool. When every hit connects and a single failed STR save can take you out, you think twice before drawing steel.

## HP Is Armor, Not Health

Cairn's HP represents your ability to avoid serious harm — your luck, your reflexes, your will to keep going. When HP hits zero, the next damage goes directly to your STR score. Take STR damage and you make a Critical Damage save. Fail, and you're out of the fight.

This means HP works more like a buffer than a health bar. A character with 1 HP isn't limping and bleeding — they're exhausted, off-balance, running out of room to dodge. The fiction stays grounded because the mechanics map to something you can describe without a medical textbook.

## Morale Checks Fix Encounter Design

Enemies in Cairn make morale checks. When a side takes their first casualty, or when they're reduced to half strength, they roll. Fail, and they flee or surrender. This one rule solved a problem I'd been fighting for years in other systems: encounters that drag on past the point of fun.

Bandits scatter when their leader drops. A wolf pack retreats after losing two members. The troll offers a deal when it realizes it's outmatched. Combat ends when the fiction says it should, not when every stat block hits zero HP.

It also changed how I design encounters. I stopped thinking about challenge ratings and started thinking about motivations. Why is this creature fighting? What would make it stop? A hungry predator behaves differently from a territorial guardian, and morale checks make that difference mechanical.

## The Takeaway

Cairn taught me that combat design isn't about balance — it's about consequences. When every hit matters and enemies have reasons to stop fighting, players engage with the fiction instead of optimizing their action economy. They talk to the bandits. They set traps. They run away when the odds are bad.

The best fights in Cairn are the ones that don't happen.
